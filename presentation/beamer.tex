\section{Motivation}
\begin{frame}
%Zach's note: Does the existence of the teleportation slide imply that this should just
%   focus on 1WQC and measurement based?

  % One-way quantum computing, measurement based quantum computing \\
  % As opposed to circuit based quantum computing

The most widely used model of quantum computation at the present time is the
quantum circuit model.

\vspace{2em}
A set containing all single-qubit gates and at least one entangling two-qubit gate (such as the controlled-\textsc{not} or controlled-\textsc{phase}) is universal\cite{brylinski2002universal}

\vspace{2em}
The quantum circuit model provides a convenient way to think about quantum computers as a system of circuits, analogous to our classical computers

\end{frame}
\begin{frame}
A vastly different model, proposed by Briegel and Raussendorf \cite{briegel2000measurements}, demonstrated that universal quantum computation could be achieved by \textit{measurements alone!}

\vspace{2em}
This so-called cluster model or \textit{one-way quantum computer} (1WQC) relies on an entangled state of a large number of qubits or \textit{cluster state} as the resource.\\

      \begin{alertblock}{Pure Quantum Model}
        These models have no evident classical analogues and they offer a new perspective on the role of entanglement in quantum computation
      \end{alertblock}

\end{frame}

\subsection{Gates through teleportation}
\begin{frame}

If we have a pool of maximally entangled states we can apply any unitary gate $U$ to any (multi-qubit) input state $\ket{\psi}$ by measurements alone.\\

\vspace{2em}
A significant annoyance is that we do not get the exact desired result $U \ket{\psi}$ but instead get $PU \ket{\psi}$ where $P$ is some Pauli operation (on each qubit) depending on the measurement outcome\cite{jozsa2006introduction}.

\end{frame}

\section{Cluster states (CS)}

\subsection{Definition}
\begin{frame}

  \textit{Cluster states} form a class of multiparty entangled quantum states which belong to the larger set of so-called graph states. 

  \begin{quotation}
  \noindent Examples of graph states:
    \begin{itemize} 
      \item Bell states
      \item Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) states
      \item states that appear in quantum error correction
    \end{itemize}
  \end{quotation}

  Intuitively, graph states can be thought of as multi-qubit states that can be represented by a graph. 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Each qubit is represented by a vertex of the graph
    \item An edge between vertices represents an interacting pair of qubits
  \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\subsection{Properties}
\begin{frame}
The spacial layout of the graph representation of the cluster state plays a role in the computational power of that state.\\

\vspace{2em}
Operations on a linearly prepared cluster state can be efficiently simulated on a classical computer in $O(n\log ^c (1/n))$, where $n$ is the initial number of qubits, and $c$ is the cost of floating point multiplication \cite{nielsen2006cluster}.\\

\vspace{2em}
In general, measurement based models can be polynomial time reduced to the gate array model, and thus have the same power, but they are more easily parallelizable \cite{jozsa2006introduction}.

\end{frame}
